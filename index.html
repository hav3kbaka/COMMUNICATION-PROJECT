Interpersonal Relationship Analysis

Knapp’s Relationship Model Applied to Real-Life Relationships

Introduction

Interpersonal communication affects how relationships develop, change, and sometimes end. In this project, I analyzed three of my own relationships using Knapp’s Relationship Model. This model explains how relationships move through stages like initiating, experimenting, intensifying, and eventually either bonding or terminating. Communication skills such as active listening, perception checking, and nonverbal communication also play an important role throughout these stages.

Relationship 1 (Close Friend)

Beginning:
I met this friend when I was really young because our families already knew each other. At first, I didn’t really want to talk to him and avoided interacting, but my dad encouraged (forced) me to at least start a conversation. I did cry and hate him for a tiny bit, but him making me do that ultimately helped me make a lifelong friend. That first interaction was awkward and very basic, which fits the initiating stage.

Progression:
Over time, we started talking more and entered the experimenting stage. We realized we had a lot in common, especially through video games like Fortnite, Roblox, and My Singing Monsters. Playing together made it easier to communicate and build a connection. Eventually, we got to the intensifying stage where we started sharing personal thoughts and secrets. Even though a lot of communication was online, nonverbal cues like tone of voice, laughing, and reactions during games helped strengthen the friendship. We still did hangout at eachothers houses however and played those same games. Later, we reached the integrating stage where people around us saw us as close friends (our parents).

Maintenance:
Even now, we still keep in contact even though he is in college. Even now we are still close friends. We make an effort to hangout with each other whenever possible, whether that’s playing games online or meeting up in person when schedules allow. We don't have any issues with eachother and whenever we do, its just some sort of ragebait and we know neither of us are being serious and we don't invade upon each other by saying something that really shouldn't be said.

Relationship 2 (Class Friendship)

Beginning:
I met this person in one of my classes in junior high. We sat next to each other and so we started making small talk about schoolwork. This initial interaction was pretty basic and it fit into the initiating stage of Knapp's model.

Progression:
As time went on, we moved into the experimenting stage by talking more about interests and joking around during class. Eventually, we reached the intensifying stage where we trusted each other more and had deeper conversations. However, over time, we started to drift apart due to differences in opinions and priorities, which is part of the differentiating stage. We also didn’t always use perception checking, which led to misunderstandings.

Ending:
The relationship moved into stagnating, where conversations became less frequent and felt forced. After that, we entered avoiding, where we barely interacted. Eventually, the relationship ended (terminating stage). Looking back, better communication could have helped prevent it from fading out. He works at the dollar tree in clinton so I see him once in a blue moon and we maybe talk for a small minute.

Relationship 3 (Family Relationship)

Beginning:
This is a family relationship that has existed my entire life, but the way we communicate has changed as I’ve gotten older.

Progression:
When I was younger, communication was simple and mostly positive. As I grew older, we experienced more of the differentiating stage because of disagreements about responsibilities and decisions. Even though conflicts happened, we were able to work through them successfully. Active listening and “I” statements helped reduce misunderstandings, and nonverbal communication like tone and body language played a big role in how messages were received.

Maintenance:
This relationship is still very strong today. We keep it going through communication, respect, and understanding each other’s perspectives. Over time, we’ve learned how to handle disagreements in a more mature way, which has helped strengthen the relationship overall. We still do have parts where it can be immature, the other day I had bought 4 muffins and in one day, they were all gone so I hate a good amount of his snacks for payback.

Conclusion

Knapp’s Relationship Model helped me understand how relationships naturally change over time. Each of my relationships followed different paths depending on communication, effort, and life circumstances. Overall, I learned that skills like active listening, perception checking, and understanding nonverbal communication are important for maintaining healthy relationships.
